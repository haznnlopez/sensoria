Motion-Tracking Suits in Elite Sprint Training

How wearable technology helped improve sprint analysis, technique feedback, and athlete monitoring.

In elite sprinting, success often depends on very small differences in technique, timing, and body control. Because of this, coaches need tools that can measure movement more precisely than observation alone. Wearable technology, especially inertial measurement units (IMUs) and other portable sensors, has become increasingly useful in sprint training because it allows biomechanical data to be collected directly on the track rather than only in the laboratory. Research shows that wearable sensors can support both performance analysis and athlete monitoring by capturing movement-related information in real training environments.

Problem Statement

A sprint training program wanted to improve the way it analyzed athletes during acceleration and maximal-speed sessions. Coaches found that video review alone took time and sometimes failed to capture subtle technical errors, such as uneven leg action, premature upright posture, or changes in step timing caused by fatigue. The staff also needed a more consistent way to monitor athletes returning from minor injury and to track whether movement patterns stayed stable across repeated sessions. Since laboratory motion-capture systems are expensive and impractical for everyday sprint practice, the team needed a field-based alternativeng and optimization of motion patterns.

How the Technology Was Used

Athletes wore IMU-based sensors during selected sprint sessions, including block starts, acceleration drills, and maximum-velocity runs. The sensors were placed on body segments such as the ankles or lower limbs so that they could capture step timing, contact-related events, and movement patterns during sprinting. The performance staff combined these data with video clips and sprint split times to build a fuller picture of technique. This method followed current wearable-sport practice, where sensor data are not used alone but alongside other coaching tools for better interpretation.

Objectives of Wearable Devices in Sprint Training

The sprint program adopted wearable technology with four main goals:

1. To improve sprint analysis during training sessions
2. To provide more specific technique feedback to athletes
3. To monitor mechanical changes over time
4. To support athlete management, especially during fatigue and return-to-play situations

Limitations and Athlete Feedback

Although wearable technology improves sprint analysis, technique feedback, and athlete monitoring, it still has limitations. One key issue is data accuracy and consistency. Reviews of athletic wearables report problems such as inaccurate readings, weak recommendations, IMU drift, GPS limits, signal interference, and differences between devices, all of which can reduce reliability in real training settings.

Another limitation is data overload. Wearables can produce many metrics, but coaches may find it difficult to turn those numbers into useful training decisions. As bioengineer Dhruv Seshadri noted, the challenge is not just collecting data, but understanding what those data mean in the athlete’s actual activity.

Wearables can also raise comfort and acceptance issues. Some athletes find them inconvenient, uncomfortable, or distracting, while others worry about being constantly monitored and judged through their data.

Lastly, privacy and data ownership remain major concerns. Performance and biometric data are sensitive, and if they are misused or shared without proper protection, they may affect an athlete’s autonomy, opportunities, and career.

“We are trying to understand, holistically, what is going to allow them to perform at the highest level.”

— Lehigh University

Wearable technology helped improve sprint analysis, technique feedback, and athlete monitoring by giving coaches access to movement data in real training conditions. In this case, the technology made it easier to measure sprint mechanics, identify technical weaknesses, monitor trends over time, and support more informed coaching decisions. As wearable systems continue to improve, they are likely to become an even more important part of sprint-performance development.
