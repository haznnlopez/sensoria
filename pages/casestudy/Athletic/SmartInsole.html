Smart Insoles and Running Economy

How motion-tracking suits, smart insoles, and joint-angle sensors give coaches and athletes objective, real-time biomechanical data to improve form and reduce injury risk.

Running economy is the amount of energy a runner uses at a given speed. Better running economy usually means the athlete can maintain pace with less oxygen cost and less wasted motion. Wearable technologies such as smart insoles, inertial measurement units, motion-tracking suits, and joint-angle sensors are increasingly used because they can measure biomechanics outside the lab and provide feedback in real training settings. Reviews show these systems are now considered practical tools for gait analysis, workload monitoring, and field-based biomechanical assessment.

Problem Statement

A middle-distance and endurance training group wanted to improve running economy without relying only on occasional lab testing. Coaches could see broad technique problems on video, but they could not measure foot loading, contact timing, or lower-limb stiffness easily during daily sessions. They needed a field-based system that could identify inefficient movement patterns early, support technique correction in real time, and help flag biomechanical changes linked to fatigue or injury risk. Evidence from sports-wearable reviews suggests this is one of the main strengths of modern wearable systems: they allow prevention, assessment, and management of movement problems in applied sport settings.

How the Technology Was Used

The athletes used smart insoles to collect plantar-pressure and foot-strike data, along with wearable motion sensors to estimate temporal parameters and lower-limb mechanics. Smart insole reviews show these systems can measure variables such as pressure distribution, contact events, and gait asymmetries, while IMU reviews show good validity for many lower-limb running measures when compared with reference systems. Together, these devices give a more complete picture of how the athlete lands, loads, and pushes off during each stride.

Objectives of Smart Insole in Running Economy

The program adopted smart insoles, supported by other wearable sensors, with four goals:

1. Monitor running mechanics during regular training.
2. Identify factors linked to poorer running economy, such as inefficient contact patterns and excessive loading.
3. Provide real-time feedback to improve technique.
4. Detect early biomechanical changes associated with fatigue or injury risk.

Study Findings

The smart-insoles-based workflow improved the coaches’ ability to measure running mechanics in the field. Reviews of running gait wearables conclude that these devices are generally valid and reliable for assessing running gait compared with laboratory reference standards, making them useful for repeated biomechanical checks during real training rather than only in controlled testing.

The data were also useful for identifying inefficient loading and asymmetry patterns that may affect running economy over time. Reviews on in-shoe systems and sports wearables note that plantar pressure monitoring can reveal foot-strike behavior, loading asymmetries, and changes in movement strategy, all of which are relevant for both performance and injury prevention. Since running economy is influenced by biomechanics as well as shoe-athlete interaction, this kind of monitoring gives coaches a practical way to detect wasteful mechanics before they become chronic habits.

Limitations and Athlete Feedback

There are still limitations. Smart insoles are practical, but they can be harder to scale across large groups because they require fitting, calibration, and device management. Field studies also show that other wearable systems may differ substantially from smart-insoles-based measures before correction; in the 2024 comparison study, IMU-estimated contact time was 26.3% to 38.5% longer and flight time was 27.3% to 54.5% shorter than smart insole values before correction. Reviews also note broader issues such as sensor drift, data interpretation challenges, and variability between devices.

Smart insoles allow “real-time posture correction, injury prevention and rehabilitation monitoring.”

— Ohio State University

This case study shows that smart insoles can play an important role in improving the way coaches assess running economy. By capturing plantar pressure, contact timing, symmetry, and stiffness-related indicators during normal training, they help coaches move from subjective observation to objective biomechanical feedback. When combined with motion-tracking suits or joint-angle sensors, smart insoles can support better form correction, more informed shoe and training decisions, and earlier detection of fatigue-related movement changes that may contribute to injury. The strongest evidence supports their use as practical field tools for monitoring and guiding technique, even though they should still be combined with expert coaching and, when needed, laboratory testing.
