The Anatomy of a Flex Sensor: Measuring the Micro-Angles of RSI

A technical breakdown of resistive flex sensors and how bending a thin substrate translates physical strain into a data-driven "Pre-Injury Score."

Mark España
Navigation
April 11, 2026
7 min read
1,204 views

At the intersection of material science and ergonomics lies the flex sensor—a low-profile component that acts as the "nervous system" for the sensoria wrist cluster. While traditional wearables rely on accelerometers to track movement, flex sensors allow us to measure the postural state of a joint with millimetric precision.

The Physics of Resistance

A resistive flex sensor operates on the principle of variable resistance. The sensor is composed of a flexible substrate coated with a thin layer of conductive carbon particles. When the sensor is in its neutral, flat position, the carbon particles are packed tightly together, allowing electrical current to pass through with minimal resistance.

As the user bends their wrist, the substrate elongates on the outer edge. This stretching forces the carbon particles further apart, creating "gaps" in the conductive path. This physical deformation results in a measurable increase in electrical resistance.

"The body doesn't move in binary code; it moves in curves. Flex sensors allow us to digitize those curves into actionable health data."

The Voltage Divider Circuit

To translate this change in resistance into something a computer can understand, the sensor is integrated into a voltage divider circuit. By pairing the flex sensor with a static resistor of a known value, we can measure the output voltage as it fluctuates with the sensor's bend.

The onboard sensoria microcontroller converts this analog voltage into a digital value (usually 0–1023). Our algorithms then map these values to specific angles—identifying whether a wrist is at a neutral 0° or a danger-zone 45° extension.

Technical Insight

By sampling this data at 500Hz, the system can detect micro-tremors and muscle instability—early warning signs of fatigue that occur long before a person feels physical pain.

Defining the "Pre-Injury Score"

The ultimate goal of this hardware is the Pre-Injury Score. By monitoring the duration and frequency of awkward joint angles, sensoria calculates the cumulative mechanical load on the tendons.

If the angle exceeds ergonomic safety thresholds for an extended period, the software flags a high-risk score. This allows for intervention—such as a haptic nudge or a mandatory break—before Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI) becomes a clinical reality.
