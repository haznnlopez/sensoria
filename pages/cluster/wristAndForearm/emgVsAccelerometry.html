EMG vs. Accelerometry: Which Wins for Wrist Health?

Comparing the tracking of physical motion against the detection of neural intent to find the perfect balance for RSI prevention.

Mark España
Navigation
April 11, 2026
8 min read
942 views

In the development of the sensoria ecosystem, a recurring question arises: should we track the movement itself, or the biological signals that precede it? While most consumer wearables settle for one, high-performance ergonomic clusters require a hybrid approach to detect "Invisible Fatigue."

Accelerometry: Measuring the Result

Accelerometers are the workhorses of the wearable world. They measure velocity and orientation in space. For wrist health, an accelerometer tells us exactly where the hand is—tracking rapid keystrokes or the repetitive flick of a mouse.

However, accelerometry only records the result of a muscle contraction. By the time an accelerometer detects a high-velocity movement that could cause strain, the physical mechanical stress has already occurred. It is a reactive data point.

"Accelerometers show us the 'what,' but EMG shows us the 'why.' One observes the action, the other observes the effort."

EMG: Tracking the Intent

Electromyography (EMG) operates on a deeper physiological level. It measures the electrical activity produced by skeletal muscles during contraction. Crucially, EMG can detect neural intent.

When a muscle begins to fatigue, its electrical firing pattern changes long before its physical output (speed or range of motion) drops. EMG allows the sensoria cluster to see the "Invisible Fatigue"—the state where your tendons are under high tension even if your hand appears still or is moving slowly.

Technical Insight

By analyzing the Median Frequency (MDF) of the EMG signal, our system identifies when a muscle is working harder to maintain the same level of precision, triggering a "Pre-Injury" warning.

Hybrid Sensing: The sensoria Advantage

The most effective prevention happens when these two sensors work in unison. Accelerometry provides the context (e.g., "The user is typing"), while EMG provides the intensity (e.g., "The user is typing with excessive muscle tension").

This hybrid data allows us to calculate a comprehensive health score. Instead of just telling you that you've been typing for three hours, sensoria can tell you that the physiological cost of those three hours is reaching a dangerous threshold for your specific muscle recovery rate.
